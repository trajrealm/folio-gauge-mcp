# Peer Comparison Skills

## Overview
Peer analysis compares a company to direct competitors on valuation, growth, profitability, and market position. Focus: relative value (is this company cheap/expensive vs. peers?). Medium-term horizon (weeks to months).

## Identifying True Peers

### Direct Competitors
- **Same Product/Service**: Apple vs. Microsoft (software), Coca-Cola vs. PepsiCo (beverages)
- **Same Customer Base**: Enterprise SaaS vendors all competing for IT budgets
- **Same Supply Chain**: Auto suppliers compete on price and quality to major OEMs

### Excluding False Peers
- Different business models (e.g., Netflix vs. Blockbuster after pivots)
- Different geographic focus (domestic vs. international)
- Different margin profiles due to scale or strategy (discount airline vs. premium airline)

### Peer Group Size
- **Ideal**: 3-10 peers for statistical confidence
- **Too Few** (< 3): Outlier effects skew averages
- **Too Many** (> 20): Dilutes signal by including tangentially-related firms

## Relative Valuation Metrics

### P/E Ratio Comparison
- **Target P/E vs. Peer Average**:
  - P/E > Peer Average: Premium valuation. Justified if growth > peers. Risky if growth similar.
  - P/E < Peer Average: Discount valuation. Attractive if fundamentals are comparable.
- **Rule**: Buy stocks trading below peer P/E if growth/margins similar → mean reversion expected.

### PEG Ratio Comparison (More Nuanced)
- **Target PEG < Peer Average + Growth**: Undervalued growth stock
- **Example**: Target PEG 0.8 vs. peer average 1.2, both growing 15% = target is cheaper growth

### Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio
- **Less manipulable than P/E** (harder to manage revenue than earnings)
- **Use for**:
  - Unprofitable companies (P/E invalid)
  - Cyclical trough (P/E distorted by temporary earnings collapse)
  - Quick comparison across peers
- **Interpretation**: P/S < 1.0 often = value. P/S > 3.0 = premium.

### EV/EBITDA (Enterprise Value / Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, Amortization)
- **More comparable than P/E across different capital structures**
  - Removes impact of debt/leverage differences
  - Removes impact of depreciation policy differences
- **Peer Comparison**: Target EV/EBITDA vs. peer average
  - Lower = potentially cheaper (but watch quality)
  - Higher = premium (justified if margins/growth superior)

## Profitability Comparison

### Gross Margin
- **Peer Average**: If target < peer avg, check for: cost inflation, pricing power loss, supply chain issues
  - Red flag: All peers at 45% margin, target at 40% = competitive disadvantage
- **Improving Margin**: Target margin rising while peers flat/declining = operational excellence or price increases
- **Declining Margin**: Target margin falling = pressure (competition, costs)

### Operating Margin (EBITDA Margin)
- **Peer Comparison**: If target < peer avg by > 5%, investigate why
  - Product mix (target sells lower-margin products)
  - Scale disadvantage (target smaller, higher overhead)
  - Geographic mix (target in lower-margin regions)
- **Margin Compression Risk**: If target margin trending down while peers stable, concern.

### Net Margin
- **Bottom-line comparison**: After all costs. Most important for profitability assessment.
- **Peer Divergence**: If target net margin >> peer avg, sustainable competitive moat OR temporary advantage?

## Growth Comparison

### Revenue Growth vs. Peers
- **Outgrowing Peers** (target 20% vs. peer avg 10%):
  - Positive: Gaining market share, superior product, better execution
  - Investigate: Is growth sustainable? Market share gains from acquisition or organic?
- **Growing Slower than Peers** (target 5% vs. peer avg 12%):
  - Negative: Losing share OR selective focus on lower-growth segments
  - Red flag: If combined with margin compression

### EPS Growth vs. Peers
- **Higher EPS Growth**: Could be from earnings management, share buybacks (financial engineering) rather than operating growth
  - Compare to revenue growth: If EPS growth >> revenue growth, investigate
- **Lower EPS Growth**: May reflect reinvestment or dilution (stock options). Context matters.

## Market Position & Share

### Market Capitalization Ranking
- **Largest Peer**: Leader, typically more stable, lower risk
- **Mid-Tier Peer**: Growth opportunity, less dominant
- **Smallest Peer**: Disruption risk, but potential acquisition target

### Market Share Trends
- **Gaining Share**: Target winning competitive battles. Positive signal.
- **Losing Share**: Target falling behind. Negative signal unless deliberate strategy.

## Competitive Moat Assessment

### Durable Advantages
- **Brand**: Apple, Coca-Cola command premium pricing despite competition
- **Switching Costs**: Enterprise software (Salesforce, Workday). High cost to switch → sticky revenue
- **Network Effects**: Linkedin, Uber. More users = more valuable to all
- **Proprietary Technology**: Pharmaceuticals with patent protection

### Vulnerable Positions
- **Commodity Product**: Indistinguishable from peers. Price only differentiator.
- **Technology Disruption Risk**: Kodak (film → digital), Blockbuster (rental → streaming)
- **Customer Concentration**: If top 3 customers = 50%+ revenue, risk if customer churns

## Scoring Guidelines

- **Strong Buy**: Cheaper valuation than peers (P/E, PEG, P/S) + superior growth OR improving margins
- **Buy**: Fair valuation vs. peers + stable/improving fundamentals
- **Hold**: Trading at peer average valuation, stable business, no catalysts
- **Sell**: Premium valuation to peers + inferior growth OR declining margins
- **Strong Sell**: Significantly expensive vs. peers + deteriorating fundamentals + losing market share

## Sector Context
- **Adjust Benchmarks by Sector**:
  - Software: High P/E (30+), high margins (40%+)
  - Retail: Low P/E (10-15), low margins (2-5%)
  - Banks: Moderate P/E (8-15), moderate margins (20-30%)
- Comparing software stock to retail stock is apples-to-oranges (use sector-specific peers)
